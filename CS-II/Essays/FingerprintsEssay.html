Fingerprints

Everyday billions of people use the internet everyday. It has been almost forty years since the Internet was officially launched. It has evolved greatly since its creation with millions of users relying on it everyday to accomplish their tasks. As more, and more people began accessing the internet, users wanted their information to be secure and private. Website owners have been switching from HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol) to HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure). The two main purposes of using HTTPS is that any data sent back and forth over these secure connections are encrypted by technology that no one knows how to break and penetrate into personal information. Companies/organizations use HTTPS Proxy Applications to log and track user activity. They install a commercially available "SSL Proxy '' and install CA (Certificate Authority) to each of its devices in the network. Therefore, the website nor the user don’t know that the information is not secure when passing through the remote web server (Gibson, 2020). A Certificate Authority (CA) is a company that validates the identities of organizations to make sure that they are genuine. Furthermore, they give the applicants receive cryptographic keys (including a public and private key) to authenticate the entity, encrypt personal data/information, and provide them with digitally signed certificates to make sure a third person does not alter information. A school/government/ISP/companies should not have a right to eavesdrop on people’s communications because it would penetrate citizens' privacy. Personal information could fall into the wrong hands and be used/manipulated in a fraudulent manner.

As more people are using the internet than ever before, people are entering sensitive information (ex: credit card information information when shopping online). Cyberattacks are increasing rapidly in today’s digital world. Cybercriminals want to “steal” sensitive information from users, businesses, and companies and to be later sold on the dark web for huge sums of money. One of the earliest forms of cyber attacks is MITM (Man In The Middle) is when an attacker intercepts data traffic between two people either to eavesdrop or manipulate the information. The attacker can alter a website’s information or redirect the user to a different one that looks exactly as the previous to capture user’s login credentials and account information (Venafi, 2022). Also, an SSL interception by an organization (ex: work/school) can be detected when the fingerprints (website certificate) provided by the proxy and from the remote web server are different. SSL interceptions cannot be prevented as there are new techniques that hackers are using each day to intercept data that is going through websites. Cryptographic hashes are used to “fingerprint” the certificates and are complex mathematical algorithms that store all information of the certificate. The essential qualities that make a hash a “good” are that if any bit of the certificate is changed, then the whole hash algorithm is changed. Sometimes, false-positives happen when there are huge websites like Microsoft and Amazon when there are different servers and domains that have different fingerprints for each of its website certificates. This leads to users to think that their connection is being intercepted but the truth is that they are just seeing a different certificate. A false-negative happens when the authentic fingerprint and the one obtained from the browser is the same but in reality, one of the websites is a fake website.
