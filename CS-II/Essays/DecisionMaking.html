Decision Making

Introduction

Millions of people around the world experience some sort of decision bias without even noticing it. Decision bias is often described as making decisions in an irrational or flawful way that could alter the outcome of a situation. This term was first coined by two Israeli-American psychologists, Daniel Kahneman, and Amos Tversky to explain the systematic thinking process that people undergo when forming decisions which sometimes is derived from distorted patterns of judgment (Ramachandran, 2012). There are many types of decision biases that exist in the world which affect our day-to-day decisions.

Anchoring Bias

One of the biggest decision making biases that I believe influences almost everybody in the world including me is anchoring bias. It is where people rely very highly on the first piece of information that is given to them to finalize their choice (Anchoring Bias, n.d.). People may not look at their other options and hastily come to their conclusion. This mostly likely occurs when a person is in a time crunch or feels stressed (8 Types of Bias in Decision Making , 2022). An example of anchoring bias would be when you are shopping for a gift for your brother’s birthday, and the first thing you see is a science book that costs $50 but you decide to put it back on the shelf as you cannot afford it as it is higher than your planned budget. Then, you look at a LEGO set that costs $35, and you purchase thinking that it is cheaper than the previous item even though it is still somewhat higher than your planned budget. Furthermore, this tactic is heavily used in the marketing industry to entice customers to purchase their products. For example, a store increases the price of a computer from $400 to $800 and then puts a sign that says “All products are marked 50% off than their respective MSRP/regular price”. Customers then believe that they are getting an awesome discount on their favorite product when the actual price of the product is the sale price of the product. This misconception can be overcome by comparing the price with other retailers, examining the product specifications, and asking friends for advice. Also, anchoring bias could also hinder our decision making skills during the course of the ISP as team members could rely strongly on the first piece of information they found on the web to assist with the design ideas of the Cafe Project. This obstacle can be tackled by ensuring that research is thoroughly done considering the positives and negatives of the choices.

Bandwagon Effect

In addition, another common type of decision bias that people face is bandwagon effect. It is where people tend to adapt trends or choices that are widely favored by others even when it goes against their beliefs or opinions (Bandwagon Effect, 2020). The origin of the term dates back to the 1800s when bandwagons were used for people to jump aboard and to enjoy the music with others. Furthermore, this process becomes ‘infectious’ with people getting on the bandwagon exponentially (Bloom, 2017). For example, voters sometimes vote for a candidate in an election that is having promising results in recent polls rather than a candidate that they associate themselves with as they want to fit in and be on the winning side. In terms of the ISP, my teammates and I could design features that are already popular instead of developing new innovative tools that could benefit users. In order to tackle this bias, my team and I should be willing to take risks and at the same time be considerate of users’ needs. We need to be innovative and original while respecting the technological trends that are shaping the industry.

Confirmation Bias

Furthermore, another decision-making bias that affects people in day-to-day life is confirmation bias. It is when people tend to support or seek information that embraces previous beliefs (Cherry, 2022). Like many cognitive biases that are present, this was first seen by the ancient Greeks thousands of years ago with Greek historian Thucydides describing in his The History of the Peloponnesian War that it is a habit of human nature for them to immerse in ideas that favor their views (Alder, n.d.). For example, many people tend to watch news outlets that support their political ideology. This practice can be detrimental as individuals are then blindsided to one perspective of the information instead of the overall issue. If too many people become closed-minded, it could lead to nationalistic or fascist parties that could lead to end of democracy. In addition, one aspect that could hinder our ISP is when during the testing phase of the project when we don't do enough testing as throughout the project, we receive good compliments about our product making us not want to listen to negative comments. This bias could be minimized by being open-minded to the other side and by taking their suggestions seriously.

Ambiguity Effect

Also, another decision-making bias that is prominent in our life is the ambiguity effect. It is when people tend to avoid choices that either lack credibility or information, wanting to choose something where there is a favorable result. This term was coined by Daniel Ellsberg while completing his Ph.D. who was completing his PH.D. in economics in 1961 in which he described that humans often don’t like to go with choices that involve uncertainty (Ambiguity Effect: Avoiding Decisions From Uncertainty, n.d.). It is often that we don’t want to take risks and thereby forcing us to make other decisions. For instance, a high school student might want to take a drawing class instead of a painting class because there is more information regarding the course (syllabus, activities, projects). This bias can be tackled by schools devising an Academic Planning Guide (APG) that details course information for each of the classes that is being offered to the students to remove ambiguity. In terms of the ISP, there might be a situation where we decide to not implement a feature as there is not much material available on the Web to learn about how to code the specific element in the application. But, this bias can be addressed by ensuring that thorough research is done in all areas including but not limited to books, videos, and Internet. It is also important to note that even if we truly find there is a lack of information, it is in the best interest for all of us to ask for help and guidance whether it may be teachers, friends, or parents.

Affect Heuristic

Finally, another decision-making bias that takes prominence in day-to-day life is affect heuristic. It is when people tend to make decisions that are based on our emotions rather than actual information or facts which most of the time result in substandard results. The bias was first thought up by a psychology professor at University of Oregon in 2000 in which he presented lab experiments and data supporting how emotions influence our behavior in turn affecting our decision-making skills (Affect Heuristic, n.d.). It is often that our emotions get enlarged enough that our brain overrides our intellectual processing skills which in turn result in hastily made decisions. For instance, you decide to sing at a school concert next Saturday and the Thursday before, you get a grade back for one of the tests you took, in which you see that you have received a bad grade, and decide to tell your teacher that you won’t be able to make it to the concert. This is not a good decision as there is no correlation between the test grade and your performance at the concert. In terms of the ISP, there could be a situation where my team and I could stop working on the project since we were not able to figure out how to fix a bug in our code. This bias could be minimized by being more patient, thinking logically, and considering all the choices available to tackle before immediately going into a mental breakdown.

Conclusion

In conclusion, these decision-making biases are just one of the hundreds present in this ‘digital’ world. It is necessary for us to realize these types of biases and rectify them before they start to exert their influence on us. So, from now onwards, let’s try to make well-thought through and unbiased decisions as much as possible.
